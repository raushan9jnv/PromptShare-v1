Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting is one of the most powerful techniques in prompt engineering. By asking the model to **show its reasoning**, you can dramatically improve accuracy on complex tasks - often by **40% or more**.

## What Is Chain-of-Thought Prompting?

Instead of asking for a direct answer, you instruct the model to think through the problem step by step before giving its final response.

### Without CoT (Standard Prompting)

```text
Q: A store has 25 apples. They sell 13, then receive a shipment of
   30 more. They give away 8 to employees. How many apples do they have?
A: 34
```

### With CoT

```text
Q: A store has 25 apples. They sell 13, then receive a shipment of
   30 more. They give away 8 to employees. How many apples do they have?

Let me work through this step by step:
1. Start: 25 apples
2. After selling 13: 25 - 13 = 12 apples
3. After shipment of 30: 12 + 30 = 42 apples
4. After giving away 8: 42 - 8 = 34 apples

A: 34 apples
```

While both get the right answer here, CoT becomes critical for harder problems where models often make errors without explicit reasoning.

## Three Flavors of CoT

### 1. Zero-Shot CoT

The simplest form - just add **"Let's think step by step"** to your prompt:

```text
Determine whether this argument is logically valid.
Let's think step by step.

"All cats are mammals. Some mammals are pets.
Therefore, all cats are pets."
```

This single phrase improves accuracy on reasoning tasks by **20-30%** across models.

### 2. Few-Shot CoT

Provide examples of reasoning chains:

```text
Q: Is 127 a prime number?
A: Let me check. I need to test divisibility by primes up to sqrt(127) ~= 11.3.
   127 / 2 = 63.5 (not divisible)
   127 / 3 = 42.3 (not divisible)
   127 / 5 = 25.4 (not divisible)
   127 / 7 = 18.1 (not divisible)
   127 / 11 = 11.5 (not divisible)
   Since 127 is not divisible by any prime <= 11, it is prime. Yes.

Q: Is 91 a prime number?
A:
```

### 3. Self-Consistency CoT

Generate **multiple reasoning paths** and take the majority answer:

```text
Solve this problem 3 different ways, then give me the answer that
appears most frequently:

"If a train travels 120km in 1.5 hours, and then 80km in 1 hour,
what is its average speed for the entire journey?"
```

## When to Use CoT

| Task Type | CoT Benefit | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Math & Logic** | Very High | Word problems, proofs |
| **Code Debugging** | High | Finding bugs, optimization |
| **Analysis** | High | Comparing options, evaluations |
| **Creative Writing** | Low | Stories, poems |
| **Simple Q&A** | Minimal | Facts, definitions |

> **Rule of thumb:** If a task requires more than one mental step, CoT will likely improve results.

## Advanced: Tree-of-Thought

Tree-of-Thought (ToT) extends CoT by exploring **multiple reasoning branches**:

```text
Consider 3 different approaches to solve this problem.
For each approach:
1. Describe the strategy
2. Work through it step by step
3. Evaluate if the result makes sense

Then select the best approach and explain why.

Problem: Design a database schema for a social media app that needs
to support posts, comments, likes, and follower relationships while
optimizing for read-heavy workloads.
```

## Practical Tips

1. **"Think step by step"** works across all major models
2. **Longer reasoning is not always better** - encourage concise, focused steps
3. **Combine with role-setting** - "You are a careful mathematician. Think through each step..."
4. **Use for verification** - "Check your answer by working backwards"
5. **Format the output** - "Show your reasoning in numbered steps, then give the final answer on its own line"

## Key Takeaway

Chain-of-thought prompting is not just a trick - it fundamentally changes how the model processes your request. By making reasoning explicit, you give the model a **scratchpad** that reduces errors and improves complex problem-solving.

Start adding "Let's think step by step" to your harder prompts today. The improvement will be immediate and measurable.
